Exploration of Human Design with Genetic Algorithms as
Artistic Medium for Color Images

The University of Kansas School of Engineering

Genetic algorithms are evolutionary algorithms that mimick the process of natural selection. "Children," or chromosomes, are considered as candidate solutions of a designated "fitness function." In this case, the children I created were 736-bit binary strings. Bits within the sequence identified characteristics such as a certain shape, color, size, etc. Each child represented one canvased artwork with 32 shapes at 23 bits per shape. I engineered the fitness function to evaluate each canvas with a variety of design elements (as color harmony, symmetry, and shape diversity). Each element could be weighted more or less heavily to promote or demote certain visual aspects of a painting. Optimal solutions identified by the fitness function are given a higher score and also a higher chance of reproducing. Children are mutated and evolved through hundreds or thousands of iterations to arrive at one optimal solution which is virtually drawn, saved, and outputted as a PNG.

After implementation, I presented my findings underscoring the continued value of genetic algorithms as a medium for user-guided creative expression. The rise of gradient-based optimization has resulted in more limited use-cases of GAs, but genetic algorithms still have applications in various settings such as hyperparameter tuning, mathematical optimization, reinforcement learning, and black box scenarios. Neural networks are favored presently in image generation due to their pattern recognition and ability to produce new content; however, in cases where a user is seeking to implement their own vision through careful algorithmic refinement, genetic algorithms still find a place in visual computing.
